The Purification ending is triggered by progressing the Aromatic Flower side-quest chain — speaking to Emma after defeating the Great Shinobi Owl, eavesdropping on her conversation with the Sculptor at the Dilapidated Temple, revisiting Hirata Estate with the Father's Bell Charm, defeating Owl (Father), and acquiring the Everblossom — and then offering both the Everblossom and the Divine Dragon's Tears to Kuro at the final-boss prompt after defeating Isshin, the Sword Saint.

The ending cutscene depicts Wolf using the Aromatic Flower to absorb the Dragon's Heritage from Kuro into himself, then severing his own immortality with the Mortal Blade — sacrificing his life so that Kuro can live as a normal mortal. The closing frames depict Kuro growing up free of the Heritage in Emma's care, with Wolf's grave tended at the Dilapidated Temple.

This is the "good ending" of the four by community consensus: Kuro survives, the Heritage is destroyed cleanly, the Sculptor's foreshadowed concern about Shura is resolved positively, and the player character's sacrifice is the canonical inversion of the Immortal Severance ending's outcome. The Purification branch is also the most narratively complete demonstration of the in-universe rules that the game has been establishing about the Dragon's Heritage, the Mortal Blade, and the Sculptor's backstory.

Trivia: the Everblossom-based purification mechanic is foreshadowed across the entire run — Senpou Temple monk dialogue, Sculptor workshop carvings, Emma's herbalist sub-quest, and the Hirata Estate audio motif — and the consistency of these references is one of the most cited examples of FromSoftware's environmental storytelling in the game.
