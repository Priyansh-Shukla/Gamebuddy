Game: Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice (sekiro)

<game_overview>
Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice is a 2019 action-adventure game by FromSoftware, set in a reimagined Sengoku-era Japan circa 1500s. The player controls Wolf, a one-armed shinobi sworn to protect a young lord named Kuro, the "Divine Heir" whose immortal Dragon's Heritage is coveted by the warring Ashina clan and the encroaching Interior Ministry. After a prologue ambush in which the antagonist Genichiro Ashina abducts Kuro and severs Wolf's arm, the protagonist is resurrected and outfitted with a Shinobi Prosthetic by an enigmatic Sculptor at the Dilapidated Temple, which becomes the central hub.

Unlike previous FromSoftware titles, Sekiro emphasizes deflection-based swordplay and posture management over stat-based RPG progression. Combat revolves around breaking an enemy's Posture meter through aggressive parries and counters rather than whittling down health. Stealth, verticality (via a grappling hook), and a rotating roster of Prosthetic Tools (Flame Vent, Loaded Axe, Shuriken, Mist Raven, and others) are central to traversal and crowd control. The player's central resource for permanent advancement is Skill Points (spent on combat arts and passive nodes), Prayer Beads (four combine into a Prayer Necklace that raises Vitality and Posture), and Gourd Seeds (which expand Healing Gourd capacity).

The world is a loosely interconnected network of regions radiating outward from Ashina Castle: the entry road of Ashina Outskirts, the optional childhood memory of Hirata Estate, the snowbound monastery of Senpou Temple atop Mt. Kongo, the chasm-spanning Sunken Valley, the swampbound Ashina Depths and Mibu Village, and the otherworldly Fountainhead Palace. Late in the game a single choice on the Castle rooftop branches the story decisively, locking the player into one of four endings (Shura, Immortal Severance, Purification, or Return) and gating which final bosses they will face. Progression is largely non-linear after the first major boss, but a recommended order — Senpou, then Sunken Valley, then Ashina Depths — minimizes locked-out content.

GameBuddy treats Sekiro as the v1 reference title because the developer is on a second playthrough with full spoiler tolerance: this game-context package is the canonical testbed for envelope filtering, gate semantics, and synthesis quality before extending to titles where the player has not yet seen the ending.

<entity node_id="armored_warrior" type="boss">
The Armored Warrior is a mini-boss fought on a narrow stone bridge midway through Senpou Temple. He is a Western-style suit of plate armor — an obvious anachronism, deliberately staged — wielding a massive hammer-axe and a kite shield. The bridge is the entire arena: there is no room to circle out, and the only exits are the two ends.

The fight is unusual because the Armored Warrior cannot be Deathblowed in the normal way. Posture-breaking him does not produce a sword-thrust kill; instead, the prompt is to kick him backward off the bridge. The player must work his Posture to full while keeping enough room behind him for the final kick to actually send him over, which makes positioning more important than damage. His hammer attacks are slow but devastating, and his shield blocks normal sword swings outright — the Loaded Axe prosthetic is the cleanest counter, breaking his guard and opening punish windows.

The fight is also the first encounter in the game with a clearly non-Japanese enemy design, and the in-universe explanation is folded into the surrounding lore: he is a foreign retainer of the monks, sent west to seek the Dragon's Heritage and now bound to its defense. Defeating him yields a Prayer Bead and the Breath of Life: Light skill.

Trivia: the plate-armor silhouette draws on late-medieval European harness rather than any Sengoku-era armor, and the kick-off-the-bridge resolution is a direct mechanical homage to a famously meme-able dueling tactic from earlier Soulsborne titles.
</entity>
<entity node_id="ashina_castle" type="area">
Ashina Castle is the central hub area of the mid-game, accessed through the great gate after the death of Gyoubu Oniwa. It is a vertical fortress of tiled rooftops, sloped courtyards, and lantern-lined corridors built into the side of a mountain. The castle introduces the first stretch of the game where verticality and grapple traversal genuinely matter — much of it is navigated by hooking between rooftops, dropping into open windows, and stealth-Deathblowing patrolling samurai from above.

Mechanically, the castle is dense with content. It contains multiple Sculptor's Idols, a number of patrolling mini-bosses (most notably the Blazing Bull on the lower courtyard and a samurai general at the main entrance), several merchants, and several pivotal NPCs. The Tengu of Ashina appears on a rooftop and offers a "rat-hunting" side quest that unlocks the Ashina Arts skill tree. Kuro is held in the topmost tower of the castle and is the player's main objective.

The castle is also the structural fulcrum of the entire game. After defeating Genichiro on its rooftop, three previously inaccessible regions open simultaneously: Senpou Temple (via a kite-zipline from a nearby tower), Sunken Valley (via a tunnel beneath the castle), and Ashina Depths (via an underground passage in the Abandoned Dungeon). The player will also return to the castle a third time, much later, with all idols grayed out and the keep in flames, for the late-game finale.

Trivia: the architecture of Ashina Castle is closely modeled on Matsumoto Castle in Nagano, including the distinctive black exterior, the steep stone foundation, and the multi-tier hip-and-gable roof line.
</entity>
<entity node_id="ashina_depths" type="area">
Ashina Depths is the swampbound, lantern-lit region beneath the castle, accessed through a long tunnel reached either from the Abandoned Dungeon or from a passage near the Sunken Valley boundary. It is a network of mist-shrouded paths through poisoned marshes, half-submerged shrines, and broken bridges, populated by red-eyed dogs, Mibu villagers, and pitch-dark caves filled with crawling apparitions.

The Depths is the structurally darkest of the three mid-game regions, both atmospherically and in terms of player threat density. Poison swamps inflict status buildup across multiple corridors, and an early section requires holding breath underwater to traverse safely; the area as a whole rewards careful pacing and route-planning over aggressive engagement. The path forks into the entrance of Mibu Village and a return-trip arena where the Headless Ape is fought after the events of Sunken Valley.

Two recurring side NPCs make their second appearances here: the Hunchbacked Sculptor (a vendor with rare consumables) and a Mibu villager merchant in a riverside hut. The Depths also contains a major optional NPC questline involving Anayama the Peddler, whose late-game branch in the area unlocks rare upgrade materials.

Trivia: the persistent green-purple color palette of the Depths is consistent with the game's visual language for Mibu-affiliated supernatural content, and the area is where the in-universe rejuvenating waters legend most directly connects to its corrupted source.
</entity>
<entity node_id="ashina_outskirts" type="area">
Ashina Outskirts is the first proper play area of the game, accessed from the Dilapidated Temple. It is a winding road of cliffside villages, abandoned checkpoints, and bombed-out fortifications that climbs from the valley floor up to the great gate of Ashina Castle. The Ministry's siege has begun in earnest: most of the soldiers patrolling the road wear the broad straw hats and red sashes of Ashina troops, but artillery, scattered corpses, and burned wagons make it clear the defense has been overrun in places.

The area teaches the core combat lessons. Outlying samurai and ashigaru drill the player on deflection timing. A nameless Tutorial-grade samurai near the first idol explains the Mikiri Counter, which is mandatory for surviving thrust attacks throughout the game. The road also contains two scripted ambush points — the Chained Ogre cliff and the General Naomori Kawarada camp — that gate progress and reward the player with the first Prayer Beads of the run.

Beyond its tutorial function, Ashina Outskirts is a recurring location: the player will return here in the late game (under different conditions and lighting) for the Demon of Hatred encounter if they pursue a non-Shura ending. The peasant NPC near the first idol, Inosuke's mother, gives the player the Young Lord's Bell Charm after a brief side conversation; this charm, offered at the Dilapidated Temple's Buddha, unlocks the optional Hirata Estate memory.

Trivia: the area's musical motif, a low, mournful biwa line, recurs in modified form in nearly every Ashina-affiliated area of the game and is one of the most consistent through-lines in composer Yuka Kitamura's score.
</entity>
<entity node_id="blazing_bull" type="boss">
The Blazing Bull is a mini-boss encountered in the lower courtyard of Ashina Castle, immediately after entering the area through the main gate. It is a massive bull with a flaming bundle of straw and metal lashed to its horns, kept in a fenced training pen by Ashina handlers. Its entire moveset is built around unpredictable horizontal charges that span the length of the arena.

The fight is one of the most-discussed early walls in the game, primarily because it doesn't reward the deflection-focused approach that the rest of the game has been teaching. The bull's charges cannot be parried; instead the player has to circle behind it after each charge ends, land two or three quick hits, and disengage before the next charge starts. Firecrackers — a Prosthetic Tool whose base item is found in the area — stagger the bull for several seconds per use, dramatically shortening the fight. Players who try to stand and trade with it typically die in seconds.

Defeating the Blazing Bull yields a Prayer Bead and Shinobi Medicine Rank 2. Beyond the rewards, the fight is one of the game's clearest demonstrations that not every encounter is a sword duel and that the Prosthetic Tool roster is meant to be used aggressively, not hoarded.

Trivia: the bull's silhouette and behavior are a reference to the legendary "fire ox" tactic attributed to Tian Dan during the Warring States of ancient China, in which oxen with flaming horns were sent to break enemy lines — adapted here into Sengoku-era visual idiom.
</entity>
<entity node_id="chained_ogre" type="boss">
The Chained Ogre is a mini-boss encountered partway up the road of Ashina Outskirts, restrained at the top of a stone staircase by two priests holding spiked rods to his neck. Killing the priests first is the recommended approach: doing so lets the player skip his throw-heavy opening phase by initiating a stealth Deathblow on him while he is still pinned.

The fight itself is a brutal stamina test for new players. The Ogre cannot be staggered by normal sword swings, has no perilous-thrust attacks that can be Mikiri'd, and his grab attacks ignore deflection entirely. Backing off and circling is the standard response to his grab tells (he raises both hands overhead, or lunges forward with arms wide). The single most important fact about the fight is that the Ogre is afraid of fire: the Flame Vent prosthetic, obtained as a base item in Hirata Estate, makes him flinch for several seconds per application, opening huge punish windows. Players who attempt the fight without fire usually find it dramatically harder.

Defeating the Chained Ogre yields the Shinobi Medicine Rank 1 skill (improving healing potency) and a Prayer Bead. He is also the first enemy in the game that visibly teaches players a key Sekiro lesson: not every fight is won by deflection, and reading enemy attack types — perilous symbol, grab, sweep, thrust — matters as much as timing.

Trivia: the red eyes the Ogre exhibits are an early visual cue for a type of enchantment that recurs throughout the game; several other enemies share the same red-eye affliction and the same fear of fire.
</entity>
<entity node_id="corrupted_monk" type="boss">
The Corrupted Monk is the major boss of Mibu Village, fought on a long wooden bridge that spans a misted river at the village's far end. She is an enormous robed figure in a vermillion mask, wielding a naginata in three distinct stances, surrounded by a swirling mist that occasionally drops butterfly-themed illusion overlays into the arena.

The fight is a stance-shift duel. The Corrupted Monk's three primary stances each have their own attack pool — a sweeping naginata stance, a thrust-heavy stance, and a leaping illusion stance — and recognizing which is loaded is the difference between cleanly deflecting and eating a perilous attack you weren't watching for. Snap Seeds, used during her illusion phase, cancel the butterflies and produce a free Posture punish window. The fight also rewards aggressive pressure: backing off and waiting for openings is generally a losing strategy because the monk's mist-recovery passively regenerates her Posture.

Defeating her on the bridge yields a Memory and unlocks the path to Fountainhead Palace (conditional on possessing the Mortal Blade). A "true" version of this same character is fought later in Fountainhead under significantly tougher conditions; the bridge encounter is the warmup, and several attack tells are reused with tighter timing later.

Trivia: the Corrupted Monk's silhouette and naginata stances are explicitly modeled on Buddhist warrior-monk (sōhei) traditions, and the swirling mist is consistent with the game's wider visual language for unkilled apparitions tied to the Mibu Rejuvenating Waters.
</entity>
<entity node_id="divine_dragon" type="boss">
The Divine Dragon is the spectacle boss of Fountainhead Palace, fought in a cherry-blossom grove at the realm's summit. He is enormous — easily filling the visible sky — and his entire moveset is built around long-range sword swings, lightning storms, and gusting winds that strip cherry blossoms across the arena. The fight is the game's grandest visual set-piece and is structurally a puzzle boss rather than a duel.

The fight has two phases. The first is a horde phase against four lesser dragon attendants on a small platform, which serves as the lightning-counter tutorial-at-scale: every attack the attendants throw is lightning-attribute, and reflecting bolts back is the only meaningful damage path. The second phase is the Divine Dragon proper. Wolf grapples between towering withered trees that stand up out of the cherry-blossom cloud cover, evading the dragon's sword sweeps and his lightning-summon barrage; the kill condition is to wait for the dragon to call lightning, jump to a tree, grapple toward him while airborne, and stab a charged lightning bolt directly into him. Repeating this sequence three times wins the fight.

Defeating the Divine Dragon yields the Divine Dragon's Tears — the central key item for all four endings — and unlocks the late-game return trip to Ashina Castle and the Owl confrontation.

Trivia: the Divine Dragon's design — pale blue-white scales, antler-like horns, sword-as-claw silhouette — references a specific class of Japanese dragon iconography (hakuryu) associated with western migration in the country's mythological tradition, and his explicit narrative function is to mark the in-universe western origin of the Dragon's Heritage.
</entity>
<entity node_id="folding_screen_monkeys" type="boss">
The Folding Screen Monkeys are a unique boss encounter inside the Main Hall of Senpou Temple, in a candlelit chamber decorated with painted folding screens. It is technically classified as a boss in the menu, but it is structurally a stealth puzzle rather than a sword fight: four monkeys (Seeing, Hearing, Speaking, and an invisible fourth) must each be hunted down and Deathblowed, with each monkey behaving according to its named sense.

The Seeing Monkey patrols and visually tracks Wolf; staying behind cover or breaking line-of-sight is enough. The Hearing Monkey is blindfolded and reacts to footsteps and grapple sounds; walking and avoiding ringable bells in the room are key. The Speaking Monkey shouts to alert the others when it sees Wolf; killing it first or quickly removes its alert function. The fourth, Invisible Monkey, only appears once the others are dead, in a small open arena, and fights briefly as a fast melee enemy that reveals itself when struck.

Defeating all four monkeys yields a Memory and the Mortal Blade — a sword carried alongside Kusabimaru that is the in-game tool for severing immortality. The Mortal Blade is the structural key item gating Fountainhead Palace and the late-game endings; without it, the path forward closes.

Trivia: the three named monkeys are a direct visual quotation of the Three Wise Monkeys (mizaru, kikazaru, iwazaru) of Tōshōgū Shrine, and the fourth invisible monkey is the lesser-known "do no evil" companion (shizaru) sometimes added to the set.
</entity>
<entity node_id="fountainhead_palace" type="area">
Fountainhead Palace is the late-game otherworldly region accessed via a sequence that requires both the Mortal Blade and the death of the Corrupted Monk. Stepping through a transitional gate at Mibu Village's far bridge transports Wolf to a moonlit floating realm of cherry blossoms, golden carp, and an Okami court suspended above the clouds — visually distinct from every previous region of the game and tonally closer to courtly mythology than to Sengoku warfare.

The palace is structured as an upward climb through three interlocking environments: a koi-pond garden patrolled by Okami warriors and the Great Coloured Carp, an inner palace of cherry-blossom courtyards and serving women, and a final shrine atop the structure where the Divine Dragon resides. The Okami warriors are a new humanoid enemy type with long polearms and lightning-attribute attacks, and the area introduces the lightning-reversal mechanic in regular gameplay — every Okami lightning attack can be jumped and redirected, the same way Genichiro's lightning is countered.

The palace also contains the True Corrupted Monk encounter (the harder, multi-phase version of the Mibu bridge fight) and concludes with the Divine Dragon set-piece in a cherry-blossom grove at the realm's summit. Defeating the dragon yields the Divine Dragon's Tears, one of the central key items for all of the game's endings.

Trivia: the visual design of Fountainhead Palace draws heavily on Heian-era court aesthetics rather than Sengoku-era military design, marking the in-universe break between the historical-feeling lower world and the mythological upper world of the Dragon's Heritage.
</entity>
<entity node_id="genichiro_ashina" type="boss">
Genichiro Ashina is the structural midpoint of Sekiro and is widely treated as the game's "skill check." He is fought on the rooftop of Ashina Castle's central keep, in a wide arena under heavy clouds, and the encounter is the first full duel against a humanoid swordsman with the player's full kit.

The fight has three phases. The first is bow-and-blade swordplay with reliable deflection windows and clear perilous tells (a high sweep, a thrust that demands Mikiri Counter, and a famous jumping-bow shot that can be deflected back at him with the right timing). The second phase introduces a longer combo string and the iconic lightning-reversal mechanic: Genichiro periodically jumps into the air, calls down lightning, and the player must jump as he strikes, then attack while airborne to redirect the bolt and stagger him for a free Deathblow. The third phase — "Way of Tomoe" — gives him red glowing strikes and Tomoe Bow attacks that combine lightning with pressure; it is brutally fast but mechanically the same fight at a higher tempo.

Defeating Genichiro is the structural pivot point of the entire game. It yields his Memory (a major Attack Power increase), unlocks the Mushin Arts skill tree, and opens the three mid-game regions: Senpou Temple to the north, Sunken Valley to the west, and Ashina Depths beneath the castle. Players who beat Genichiro generally consider the rest of the game's mechanics to be in their hands; the fights that follow are dangerous, but they all reuse skills first asked for here.

Trivia: Genichiro's lightning-reversal mechanic is foreshadowed earlier in the game by a Sculptor's Idol dialogue and a dropped scroll, both of which describe the Lightning of Tomoe technique. He is not the final time the player will see him.
</entity>
<entity node_id="gyoubu_oniwa" type="boss">
Gyoubu Masataka Oniwa is the first true main boss of Sekiro and is the gatekeeper between Ashina Outskirts and Ashina Castle. The fight takes place on a wide, flat field in front of the castle's gate — a deliberate change of arena scale from the cramped duels that precede it. Gyoubu fights mounted on a black horse, charges with a long spear, and announces himself with a famous shouted introduction that has become one of the most quoted lines in the game.

The fight is the game's introduction to large-arena combat. Gyoubu's primary attacks are charges with a hooked spear; his perilous thrust is reliably defeated by the Mikiri Counter, which is the single most reinforced lesson of the early game. His other major opener — a horizontal sweep from horseback — must be jumped, not deflected. Players who internalize "thrust = Mikiri, sweep = jump, everything else = deflect" usually beat Gyoubu cleanly; players who try to roll out of attacks the way they would in a Souls game struggle.

Defeating Gyoubu yields his Memory (raising Attack Power) and the prosthetic upgrade material that, paired with the Mechanical Barrel found in his arena, unlocks the Loaded Spear at the Sculptor. He is the first scripted unlock point in the linear-feeling opening hours and serves as the structural divider between the tutorial-grade Outskirts content and the more open mid-game.

Trivia: Gyoubu was historically a real Sengoku-era commander serving under the Hojo clan, and his appearance in the game is a reference to that lineage; the "naginata-on-horseback" silhouette is borrowed from period scrolls depicting Hojo cavalry.
</entity>
<entity node_id="headless" type="boss">
The Headless are recurring apparition-type mini-bosses scattered through the world in dim caves, underwater pools, and other liminal spaces. The first Headless is encountered in a cave near a stream off the main path of Ashina Outskirts, behind a misty veil. They are tall, gaunt, decapitated samurai who fight in slow motion, surrounded by a Terror aura that drains Wolf's posture and eventually instakills him if the Terror meter fills.

A Headless fight is fundamentally about preparation rather than mechanics. The Divine Confetti consumable temporarily lets Wolf damage apparition-type enemies properly; without it his hits do almost nothing. The Pacifying Agent consumable slows Terror buildup, which is the difference between a winnable fight and a frantic Terror-death. The Headless's signature move — a slow, telegraphed underhand grab from a kneeling stance — is jumpable but very punishing if missed.

Defeating each Headless yields a Spiritfall consumable (Ako's Spiritfall, Ungo's Spiritfall, and variants), which functions like a temporary buff for combat or healing. They are the optional reward-engine of the game's apparition tier and are generally considered some of the most patience-testing fights in Sekiro.

Trivia: the Headless's gait and posture closely mirror traditional Noh theater stage movement, particularly the slow-stepping shite of a vengeful-ghost play.
</entity>
<entity node_id="hirata_estate" type="area">
Hirata Estate is the first memory area of Sekiro — a flashback location accessed by offering the Young Lord's Bell Charm to the Buddha statue inside the Dilapidated Temple. The memory takes place years before the main game, during a night raid on the Hirata clan's mountain estate by the bandits of Juzou the Drunkard, and Wolf revisits the night as a one-armed shinobi remembering events when he still had both arms.

Visually and structurally, Hirata is a tight stealth playground: pine forests on fire, a torchlit village, a riverside path with thatched-roof buildings, and a serene lord's residence at the back containing a hidden temple beneath. Audio cues — distant shouts, crackling flame, an isolated biwa motif — set it apart from the daylit Ashina exterior. The area introduces several systems: large groups of enemies that reward stealth-chain Deathblows, fire-element environmental hazards, and the first sustained set of NPCs with backstory dialogue.

Hirata is technically optional. A player can complete the entire main story without ever offering the Young Lord's Bell Charm. Skipping it, however, locks out the Flame Vent prosthetic base item (a crucial counter to the Chained Ogre and several other red-eyed enemies), the Shuriken Wheel upgrade material, two Prayer Beads, and the Sakura Droplet healing item. For these reasons it is universally recommended as the first detour after Ashina Outskirts.

Trivia: the Hirata clan and its young lord are the in-universe origin of Wolf's first oath of service; this memory is the canonical introduction of the character whom Kuro's storyline ultimately mirrors.
</entity>
<entity node_id="juzou_the_drunkard" type="boss">
Juzou the Drunkard is a mini-boss encountered in a torchlit courtyard partway through Hirata Estate, surrounded by roughly a dozen bandit retainers. He is enormous — easily twice Wolf's height — wields a wide-bladed cleaver, and inflicts Poison buildup with a gourd of putrid alcohol that he periodically slings outward in an arc.

The fight is balanced around the assumption that the player will not engage Juzou alone. A samurai NPC named Nogami Gensai stands at the gate to the courtyard, and ringing his bell (or speaking to him) enlists him as a temporary ally who will draw the bandits' attention and tank some of Juzou's aggression. Without the ally, the fight becomes a chaotic ten-on-one in which Poison stacking is almost guaranteed to drop Wolf before posture damage can land. With the ally and aggressive stealth on the surrounding bandits beforehand, the fight is a clean two-Deathblow duel.

Defeating Juzou yields a Prayer Bead and the Bite Down consumable. He is also the structural gate to the inner courtyard where the path forks toward Lady Butterfly's hidden temple.

Trivia: Juzou's bandit crew are the canonical raiders responsible for burning Hirata Estate the night of the memory, and his presence inside it is the in-universe reason the estate falls.
</entity>
<entity node_id="kusabimaru" type="item">
Kusabimaru is Wolf's katana and his only sword for the entire game — Sekiro has no weapon-swapping system the way other FromSoftware titles do. The blade was a gift from Kuro and is recovered by Wolf at the Dilapidated Temple after the prologue. Its name translates roughly to "wedge" or "linchpin," reflecting Kuro's intent that it bind Wolf to his service.

Mechanically, the sword's damage is fixed; it cannot be upgraded with materials in the way prosthetic tools can. Instead, Wolf's offensive power scales through Attack Power, a stat raised by defeating major bosses and consuming the Memories they leave behind. Posture damage — the more important meter in most fights — scales with both Attack Power and aggressive offense, but is dealt primarily through deflection, which costs nothing.

The blade is more than a stat stick. Several Combat Arts (Mortal Draw, One Mind, Floating Passage, Ashina Cross) are sword techniques layered on top of Kusabimaru, and the late-game Mortal Blade exists alongside it rather than replacing it. The fact that the player carries the same blade from the first hour to the final boss is part of the game's design ethos: mastery, not equipment, is the progression curve.
</entity>
<entity node_id="lady_butterfly" type="boss">
Lady Butterfly is the first major optional boss of the game, fought in a hidden temple beneath the lord's residence in Hirata Estate. She is an aged kunoichi who fights in flowing white robes, leaps between hanging streamers in the rafters, and uses an illusion-based moveset built around purple butterfly projectiles and shadow clones.

The fight is two phases. Phase one focuses on aerial leaps, a slow kunai-throw spread, and a perilous ground-slam thrust that must be Mikiri'd. Phase two introduces Illusory Butterflies — a swarm of homing damage spheres — and her Shadow Clone follow-up after she dies the first time, where she rises again wreathed in butterflies and adds an instakill grab to her repertoire. The Whistling Prosthetic Tool's analog and Snap Seeds (consumables that cancel illusions) trivialize the Shadow Clone phase if used at the right moment, which is one of the game's earliest demonstrations of prosthetic specialization mattering.

Defeating Lady Butterfly yields her Memory (Attack Power), the Sakura Droplet (a permanent expansion of Resurrection charges — among the most valuable single items in the game), and dialogue from the Sculptor afterward that significantly fleshes out his backstory and his relationship to the player character.

Trivia: Lady Butterfly was Wolf's shinobi teacher in his pre-game backstory, and her death in the memory is the canonical end of his apprenticeship.
</entity>
<entity node_id="long_arm_centipede_giraffe" type="boss">
Long-arm Centipede Giraffe is a mini-boss fought in a small chamber at the entrance to the Gun Fort, on the lower path of Sunken Valley. He is the direct mirror of Sen-Un at Senpou Temple — same robed, centipede-armed monk silhouette, same machine-gun deflection pattern, same long combo strings broken by perilous sweeps.

Mechanically the fights are essentially identical with minor timing tweaks: Giraffe's combos are slightly faster, his sweep tells are slightly shorter, and he has a single new move — a wider sidestep before reengaging — that punishes a lazy advance. Players who have already cleared Sen-Un usually defeat Giraffe in one or two attempts; players who skipped Senpou and arrive here first occasionally find Giraffe to be the harder introduction to the centipede pattern.

Defeating Giraffe yields a Prayer Bead and gates access to the Gun Fort interior, where the path to the Guardian Ape begins.

Trivia: the unusual "Giraffe" naming is a translation artifact; the original Japanese name uses a long-necked apparition descriptor that the localizers rendered with the more readable Western animal name.
</entity>
<entity node_id="long_arm_centipede_senun" type="boss">
Long-arm Centipede Sen-Un is a mini-boss fought in a small open-air pavilion within Senpou Temple. He is a robed monk whose forearms and hands have been mutated into many-jointed, claw-tipped centipede limbs. He fights as a pure-deflection skill check: rapid, machine-gun strings of identical horizontal slashes that go on for what feels like ten or fifteen swings before a single pause.

The intended play pattern is to hold the deflect button position and let the rhythm carry. There is essentially no defensive option other than deflection — his combos are too long to dodge through and too short-windowed to interrupt. A handful of his swings late in a combo include a perilous-sweep tell that must be jumped, which is the only break in the rhythm and the most common cause of failed deflection chains. Once a player internalizes the cadence, the fight becomes a pure exercise in posture management and is often cited as the cleanest deflection drill in the game.

Defeating Sen-Un yields a Prayer Bead. There is a near-identical variant, Long-arm Centipede Giraffe, located in Sunken Valley with subtly different timing; the two fights are explicit mirrors of each other.

Trivia: the Centipede affliction in Sekiro is consistently associated with consuming the centipede-form Buddhist relic kept at Senpou, and its visual motif recurs in multiple late-game enemies and items.
</entity>
<entity node_id="lose_arm" type="story_beat">
At the conclusion of the prologue, Genichiro severs Wolf's left arm at the elbow and leaves him for dead. Wolf regains consciousness in the Dilapidated Temple, a half-ruined sanctuary on a hillside outside Ashina Castle. He is now fitted with a strange wooden left arm — the Shinobi Prosthetic — by the Sculptor, a one-armed monk who maintains a workshop full of Buddha carvings inside the temple. Emma, a doctor in service to the Ashina clan, is also present and explains that Wolf has been brought back by the power of the Dragon's Heritage flowing through his pledged bond with Kuro: the Resurrection mechanic the player will use throughout the game.

The Dilapidated Temple becomes the central hub for the rest of the game. The Sculptor fits and upgrades Prosthetic Tools when Wolf returns with base items and upgrade materials. Emma provides medicinal upgrades and gradually opens up dialogue branches that hint at the player's options later in the story. Both the temple's Buddha Idol and every other Sculptor's Idol in the world serve as the game's checkpoint system — sitting at an idol restores Wolf's Healing Gourd charges but respawns most non-boss enemies.

This beat also introduces the game's core economy: Skill Points (spent on Combat Arts and passive nodes), Sen (currency), Prayer Beads (four make a Prayer Necklace, which raises Vitality and Posture), and Gourd Seeds (given to Emma to expand Healing Gourd uses). All of these are persistent across deaths, with caveats: dying without resurrecting drops half of your unspent XP and Sen, but Unseen Aid sometimes cancels that loss.

<entity node_id="mibu_village" type="area">
Mibu Village is the riverside settlement deep within Ashina Depths, reached after crossing a long bridge over a misted ravine. It is structured as a single long street of thatched-roof homes and small shrines along the bank of a slow-flowing river, ringed by paddy fields and a wooden bridge. The village is populated by hostile Mibu villagers in straw hats wielding farm implements, many of whom carry oversized rejuvenating-water gourds and chant unsettling prayers as they patrol.

The village's central plaza contains a recurring set-piece encounter against the same overworld mob across multiple intersecting streets, and the open-water approach to the temple at the river's end is one of the game's most distinct stealth-by-swimming stretches. Underwater traversal here introduces the player to deep-dive content for the first time at scale, with multiple Sculptor's Idols and prayer-bead pickups submerged in the riverbed.

The temple at the river's end is the boss arena of the Corrupted Monk, the main boss of the Depths/Mibu arc. Defeating the monk unlocks the path to Fountainhead Palace, conditional on also possessing the Mortal Blade.

Trivia: the Mibu villagers' rejuvenating-water motif is the in-universe origin of the immortality-bestowing power that Senpou Temple has been attempting to refine, and the village's eerie chanting forms one of the most quotable audio motifs in the game.
</entity>
<entity node_id="mortal_blade" type="item">
The Mortal Blade is a black-bladed sword received after defeating the Folding Screen Monkeys at Senpou Temple. It is one of the game's central key items, both narratively and mechanically: it is the in-universe instrument capable of severing the bond of immortality, and the actions the late game asks of the player almost all involve drawing it.

Mechanically the blade is not a replacement weapon. Wolf continues to carry Kusabimaru for all normal swordplay; the Mortal Blade is drawn for specific Mortal Draw combat-art animations and for scripted story Deathblows against immortal opponents. It is also the key item required to access Fountainhead Palace and to perform the final action of most endings.

The blade's effect on enemy fights is subtle: most normal enemies do not register the blade as anything special. But against certain late-game opponents — Genichiro's resurrection forms, the Headless variants, and the final-boss sequences — its presence in Wolf's inventory unlocks new prompts and changes which Deathblow option appears. Possessing it is necessary; using it well is a function of the player's Mushin Arts tree investment.

Trivia: the blade is canonically the Black Mortal Blade, paired in lore with a separately existing Red Mortal Blade carried by Emma. The two blades' relationship is referenced obliquely in Senpou Temple inscriptions and Sculptor dialogue.
</entity>
<entity node_id="mushin_arts" type="skill">
Mushin Arts is the high-tier skill tree that unlocks after defeating Genichiro Ashina. Up until that fight, the player has had access to three skill trees — Shinobi, Prosthetic, and Ashina Arts — each containing combat arts, latent skills (passive bonuses), and a small number of texts. Mushin Arts is gated behind Genichiro because it represents the techniques Wolf is presumed to have learned by surpassing the head of the Ashina school.

The tree contains the late-game Combat Arts most associated with high play: Mortal Draw (and its upgrade Empowered Mortal Draw), Floating Passage, One Mind, and Vault Over. It also contains several latent skills built around Posture damage, deflection generosity, and emblem efficiency, all of which start to matter much more in the boss density of the second half of the game.

The tree is not strictly mandatory — none of its skills are required to complete the story — but it is the source of most "build identity" choices going forward. A player who pivots heavily into Floating Passage plays a very different game from one who specializes in Mortal Draw, and the game's late bosses are tuned with the assumption that the player is meaningfully invested in at least one Mushin tree path.

Trivia: "Mushin" (無心) translates roughly to "no-mind," the Buddhist-derived swordsmanship ideal of action without conscious thought; in-universe the tree represents techniques passed down within the Ashina school's inner circle.
</entity>
<entity node_id="owl_iron_code_choice" type="story_beat">
After defeating the Divine Dragon and returning to a burning Ashina Castle, Wolf encounters his adoptive father — the Great Shinobi Owl — on the castle's central rooftop. The Owl, presumed dead since the events of the Hirata Estate memory, reveals that he has returned to seize the Dragon's Heritage for himself. He gives Wolf an ultimatum: forsake Kuro and follow the shinobi Iron Code, or break the code and stand against him.

The choice is the single most consequential decision in the entire game. Selecting "Obey your father, forsake Kuro" locks the player onto the Shura ending branch: Wolf turns against Kuro, fights Emma and Isshin Ashina in their final defense of the Divine Heir, and the run concludes with Wolf's transformation into a Shura demon. The Shura branch is significantly shorter than the alternative and cannot be combined with any of the other three endings on the same playthrough.

Selecting "Stay loyal to Kuro, break the Iron Code" opens the long path: Wolf fights the Great Shinobi Owl on the rooftop, then progresses through the late-game content — Demon of Hatred, optional Owl Father memory rematch, the Inner Sanctum aromatic quest, the Return-ending Rice and Frozen Tears quest — toward one of the three remaining endings, all of which culminate in the same final boss sequence at Ashina Reservoir.

This is the canonical "lock-in" point of the run. Save-scumming around this choice is the established community-recommended method of seeing all four endings in the minimum number of playthroughs.
</entity>
<entity node_id="prologue_genichiro" type="story_beat">
The opening sequence places Wolf — the player character, a shinobi sworn to the Divine Heir Kuro — at the bottom of a dry well in Ashina territory. After a brief tutorial pursuing intruders through a moonlit garden, Wolf reaches the upper floor of the tower where Kuro is held captive. The encounter with Genichiro Ashina that follows is the game's first scripted boss fight and is unwinnable on a first attempt; it functions primarily as a cinematic establishment of the antagonist's skill and the protagonist's place in the world.

Genichiro is presented as a master swordsman and bowman, grandson and adopted heir to Isshin Ashina, head of the failing Ashina clan. His pursuit of Kuro is motivated by the legend of the Dragon's Heritage, an immortality-conferring lineage that Kuro carries and which Genichiro believes can save his dying province from the encroaching Interior Ministry. The prologue establishes that the player will be expected to lose fights and learn from them, that posture and deflection matter more than running away, and that the central narrative tension is the tug-of-war over Kuro.

Trivia: the falling-action shot of the prologue — Wolf plummeting from the tower as Genichiro stands over Kuro — was used heavily in the game's pre-release marketing and is the first time the title's tagline "Shadows Die Twice" is contextualized, foreshadowing the Resurrection mechanic that follows.

<entity node_id="senpou_temple" type="area">
Senpou Temple, Mt. Kongo is the snowbound monastery atop the mountain to the north of Ashina Castle. It is accessed via a kite-and-zipline rigged on a tall tower in the castle area after defeating Genichiro. The area is a tiered complex of wooden cloisters, prayer halls, snow-frosted bell towers, and pine forests, populated by red-eyed monks in straw cloaks, novice acolytes, and several distinctly unsettling experimental subjects.

The temple's narrative function is to introduce the player to the in-universe history of immortality experimentation. The monks of Senpou are not merely contemplative; they have been performing increasingly grotesque rituals in pursuit of the same Dragon's Heritage that Genichiro covets, and their work has produced the Centipede affliction, the warped child-aspect monks, and the Mibu Rejuvenating Waters. The area also houses a sealed Inner Sanctum, which is the home of the Divine Child of Rejuvenation, a key NPC for one of the game's endings.

Mechanically, Senpou is widely considered the easiest of the three mid-game regions and is recommended as the first of them. Its mini-bosses (Armored Warrior and Long-arm Centipede Sen-Un) are technically demanding but rewarding to master, and clearing the area culminates in the Folding Screen Monkeys puzzle-boss, which yields the Mortal Blade — the key item gating Fountainhead Palace.

Trivia: the temple's bell, struck during the Senpou rest dialogue, gives the area its name in some translations and provides the gentle pacing of the area's score, which composer Yuka Kitamura uses to set Senpou apart tonally from the bombast of the castle.
</entity>
<entity node_id="shinobi_prosthetic" type="item">
The Shinobi Prosthetic is the wooden left arm that the Sculptor fits to Wolf after the prologue. It functions as a permanent equipment slot rather than a consumable, and it is the slot into which all Prosthetic Tools — Shuriken Wheel, Flame Vent, Loaded Axe, Loaded Spear, Loaded Umbrella, Mist Raven, Sabimaru, Divine Abduction, Finger Whistle, and others — are installed.

At any given time the prosthetic can hold three equipped tools, cycled with a single button. Tools are powered by Spirit Emblems, a consumable currency dropped by enemies and purchasable from merchants; each tool consumes one or more emblems per use. The prosthetic also enables the grappling hook, which dramatically changes traversal: any green hook icon in the world is a grapple point, and chains of grapples form the backbone of mid- and late-game vertical level design.

Tools are not just utility — many are situational counters. The Flame Vent applies Burn to fire-vulnerable enemies, the Loaded Axe breaks shield guards and high-posture stances, and Divine Abduction can spin enemies around to expose their backs for a Deathblow. The prosthetic is the single biggest expression of player loadout choice in Sekiro; experimentation with the three-slot rotation per area is the main form of build optimization.
</entity>
<entity node_id="snake_eyes_shirahagi" type="boss">
Snake Eyes Shirahagi is a mini-boss encountered in Sunken Valley, fought on a small platform near the rope bridges. She is a tall, pale-haired woman of the Okami lineage who fights with a long matchlock rifle at range, a kunai-and-grab moveset up close, and a perilous-thrust grab that drags Wolf forward and inflicts massive damage.

The fight is a hybrid of dodge-the-gun and read-the-grab. At range her rifle shots are reliably deflectable but quickly chunk Posture; at close range her grab attacks ignore Posture entirely and require either a clean dodge or an interrupting Sabimaru thrust. The most common cause of death is ignoring the grab tell while concentrating on rifle deflections. There is a second variant, Snake Eyes Shirafuji, fought in a similar pose elsewhere in the same region; the two share the moveset and differ mainly in arena.

Defeating Shirahagi yields a Prayer Bead and reinforces the area's central lesson: closing distance fast is the answer to most Sunken Valley problems, and the Loaded Umbrella is the tool that makes closing distance survivable.

Trivia: the Okami women of Mibu lineage are referenced throughout the game in scrolls and item descriptions, and Shirahagi's appearance is the first time the player sees one of them outside of legend or item text.
</entity>
<entity node_id="sunken_valley" type="area">
Sunken Valley is the cliffside chasm region accessed through a tunnel beneath Ashina Castle after Genichiro is defeated. It is a vast vertical chasm bridged by rope walkways and natural rock outcrops, with cascading waterfalls in the background and pine trees clinging to the cliff faces. The region is split into two named sub-zones: an upper Cliffside path and a lower Gun Fort, both accessed via grapple chains down the chasm walls.

The defining mechanical feature of the valley is gunfire. Gun-wielding Sunken Valley clansmen are stationed across the chasm at long range, and even passive traversal puts the player under sustained ranged fire from positions that cannot be reached without grappling. The area rewards verticality and patience: the Loaded Umbrella prosthetic, which deflects ranged shots, becomes meaningfully useful for the first time here. The Mist Raven Feathers consumable, dropped by long-range gunners, also enters wide rotation.

Two mini-bosses are encountered. Snake Eyes Shirahagi guards the path near the upper rope bridges with a long-range rifle and grab attacks. Long-arm Centipede Giraffe — the mirror of Sen-Un at Senpou — defends the entrance to the Gun Fort proper. The valley also contains the in-universe Great Serpent, a massive snake that patrols certain cliff sections and can instakill on sight, requiring stealth and grapple-based traversal.

Trivia: the cliffside positions of the gunners and the long-zoom telescope sniper near the central rope bridges are the most direct reference in the game to the genre of Sengoku-era ranged warfare that historically arose around Tanegashima matchlock rifles.
</entity>
<entity node_id="true_corrupted_monk" type="boss">
The True Corrupted Monk is the second encounter with the Mibu naginata-wielder, fought on a cherry-blossom-lined bridge in Fountainhead Palace. It is structurally a three-phase fight where the bridge encounter at Mibu was effectively one phase, and the moveset is meaningfully expanded: she retains the naginata stance shifts but adds new perilous attacks, longer combo strings, and — most dramatically — an illusory butterfly summoning ability that fills the arena with homing damage spheres mid-combo.

The first phase is recognizable from the Mibu bridge fight. The second phase opens with a Deathblow prompt that pivots cleanly into the new butterfly content; Snap Seeds again cancel the illusion swarm but are now consumed faster because she summons multiple swarms per phase. The third phase introduces a leaping flute summon that calls additional butterflies as a screen-fill projectile, on top of the existing pressure. The fight is widely considered one of the cleanest Posture-management drills in the late game, and the bridge arena's narrow shape rewards aggressive forward play.

Defeating the True Corrupted Monk yields a Memory and a Prayer Bead, and opens the path further into Fountainhead Palace toward the Divine Dragon's grove.

Trivia: the dual-character framing — Mibu monk + Fountainhead monk — is one of the game's signature uses of bridge arenas, which serve almost exclusively as boss arenas in Sekiro and recur often enough to be a recognizable design signature.
